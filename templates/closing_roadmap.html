Closing Roadmap

After your offer gets accepted, a lot starts happening at once. This page tells you who handles what, what to ask, and what not to guess on.

The main idea

You are not supposed to understand every document by yourself. Your job is to know who to ask before you sign, pay, repair, refinance, or move money around.

If something affects your loan, payment, tax bill, repair cost, or legal rights, stop and ask the right person before acting.

🏦

Lender

Loan approval, credit re-checks, employment verification, rate, monthly payment, cash to close, refinance options, and whether new debt will hurt you.

📄

Escrow

Signing appointments, closing paperwork, money collection, title/recording, and making sure the transaction closes in the right order.

🔧

Inspector / Contractor

The inspector tells you what looks wrong. A licensed contractor gives repair scope, price, permits, and whether the work is urgent or can wait.

What happens after the offer is accepted

  1. Inspection happens first

    The inspection is your chance to understand the house before you fully commit. Ask: "What is safety, what is expensive, and what can wait?"

  2. Escrow starts collecting paperwork

    Escrow is the neutral middle person. They do not decide if the house is good or bad. They move documents, money, title, and recording toward closing.

  3. The lender may re-check credit and employment

    This is normal. Do not open new credit, finance furniture, change jobs, move large money around, or pay extra debts without asking your lender first.

  4. You get final loan papers

    The Closing Disclosure is the big one. It shows your final loan terms, projected monthly payment, closing costs, and cash needed to close.

    CFPB: Closing Disclosure

  5. You ask repair questions before closing

    For construction questions, ask a licensed contractor, not Zillow, not a comment thread, and not a guess. If the repair is over your comfort level, talk to your agent before removing contingencies.

    Check Contractor License

  6. You sign, fund, record, then get keys

    Signing papers is not always the same moment as owning the house. The deal usually has to fund and record before the keys are officially yours.

Who do I ask?

Legal words in contracts

Ask your agent to explain the form. If you need legal advice, ask a real estate attorney. Escrow and agents are not your personal attorney.

Loan, payment, or refinance

Ask your lender. Then compare with another lender if the answer affects your payment for years.

Repairs or construction

Ask the inspector what is wrong, then ask a licensed contractor what it costs and whether permits are needed.

Tax, Mello-Roos, or escrow payment

Check the county tax bill, then ask your lender how taxes and insurance affect the monthly payment.

Refinance questions people always ask

  1. How soon can I refinance?

    There is no single answer for every loan. FHA streamline refinance has its own rules. Conventional, FHA, VA, cash-out, and lender overlays can all be different. Ask your lender what applies to your exact loan.

    HUD: FHA Streamline

  2. Will refinancing lower my payment?

    Only if the new loan math works. A lower rate can help, but closing costs, loan term, taxes, insurance, mortgage insurance, and rolled-in fees can eat the savings.

  3. What if I take cash out?

    Cash-out refinance means you borrow more against the house. That can raise your payment, restart your loan term, and put your equity at risk. Ask for the before-and-after payment in writing.

  4. What question should I ask before refinancing?

    Ask: "What is my current payment, new payment, total closing cost, new loan balance, break-even point, and total interest if I keep the loan?"
